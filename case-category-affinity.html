Case 01 · Bloomreach · Ecommerce

Same list, same send. A personal category signal did the rest.

A music-instrument & pro-audio retailer turned the behavioural data already flowing into Bloomreach into a per-customer category signal. In a controlled A/B — with opens held level — click-through rose from 1.32% to 1.49%, and revenue per subscriber followed.

The kind of work three teams tend to ask for at once: teams who've just adopted a platform and need it to start producing, scaling teams whose retention revenue has plateaued, and in-house teams with the ideas but not the modelling bandwidth. One signal served all three.

Context

A capable platform, a big base — and generic sends

The retailer had everything a modern lifecycle program needs: a large, engaged subscriber base and a capable engagement platform in Bloomreach. What it didn't have was a reason for each customer to click. Sends went out one-size-fits-all, and revenue-per-send had settled into a plateau — the base was being emailed, but not read.

The behavioural signal to do better was already there — every view, cart and purchase flowing into the platform. It just wasn't being turned into anything a customer could feel.

Challenge

The data existed. The signal didn't — and neither did the spare bandwidth

Three things stood between the base and the revenue in it. The personalization logic had never been built. A real data gap sat underneath it — the cart event didn't carry its category, so a key intent signal was missing. And the in-house team could run business-as-usual, but not stand up behavioural modelling on top of it without dropping everything else.

So we set out to prove a few things, deliberately, before scaling anything.

1

If each send leads with the categories a customer actually shows affinity for, click-through rises — without changing send volume or leaning on opens.

2

The behavioural events already in Bloomreach carry enough signal to personalize well — no new platform, no new data contract, no new headcount.

3

A disciplined rotation keeps it fresh, so the lift holds as it scales across markets rather than fading after the novelty.

Approach

A category affinity signal that learns each customer's taste — and keeps up

We built the signal on the data already there. Nothing new to buy, nothing new for the team to learn. Three moving parts, and a data gap fixed along the way.

1

Reads intent

A purchase says more than a cart; a cart says more than a view. Each event is weighted by how much intent it really carries.

2

Stays current

It leans on the last 30 days, so the signal moves as taste moves — this season's interest, not last year's.

3

Rotates cleanly

Two personal categories per send on a fixed multi-week rotation, with dedup, quality guardrails, and a sensible default for cold profiles.

Built entirely inside Bloomreach. No new platform, no new headcount — and we closed the data gap by reconstructing the missing cart-event category, so the intent signal was complete.

Result

Proven small, then it scaled

  • The controlled test. On a mid-sized audience split in two, with opens held level, click-through rose from 1.32% to 1.49% — and among the people who opened, clicks climbed from 2.7% to 3.1%. On the buyers in that first send, revenue per thousand subscribers moved from roughly €41 to €99: directional, given the small pool of buyers, but pointing firmly the same way.
  • It held as it grew. Run again the next week on a larger, multi-market audience, the lift showed up in the majority of markets — for instance 1.8% to 1.9% in one and 2.6% to 2.8% in another. Openers behaved the same on both sides; where a market moved differently, we learned from it rather than looked away.
  • It won the month in production. Live, the signal powered two sends — one small-scale, one a large-scale flagship. Both became the best-performing campaigns of the month, at 2.2% and 2.7% click rates against a 0.4–1.2% norm. Modeled at a typical €200–300 order value, that points to roughly €80–120 revenue per thousand subscribers on the affinity sends — several times an ordinary send. (Illustrative model; the measured figure is the €41 → €99 above.)
Proof — the hypotheses, closed

What the test actually settled

Hypothesis 1 — confirmed

Measured against a control, with opens held equal, the +13% click-through is attributable to the content, not the reach. Leading with real category affinity moves clicks.

Hypothesis 2 — confirmed

It ran entirely on events already in Bloomreach — no new platform, no new data contract, no new headcount. The signal was in the base all along.

Hypothesis 3 — confirmed

The rotation held up as it scaled across markets, and the personalized flagship became the top send of the month after rollout — evidence it keeps working, not just launches well.

Kept honest: click-through is the robust, controlled result. The purchase, AOV and revenue-per-subscriber figures are directional — real, but drawn from a small first-test base — so we scope them to that first send rather than present them as a steady-state guarantee.
